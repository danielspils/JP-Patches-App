---
title: "Missing Music: Troubleshooting Tape Dumps"
date: 2026-06-28
---

JP Patches is a program I've wanted to build since the 1990s. I bought the JX-3P in 1984 as a sophomore in high school — a 32-patch polyphonic synthesizer that stored its sounds the way computers of its era stored data: by screaming them onto cassette tape, modem-like, through a 1/4" audio jack. By the time personal computers grew powerful enough to do anything interesting with this idea, decades had passed. The cassettes had grown brittle. The tape decks had vanished. The sounds and sequences I'd programmed since high school were gone forever. "Lost in time, like tears in rain."

With the advent of AI I saw an opportunity. I talked to a few dev friends and they pointed me to Claude Code. Then, I started talking to Claude. Within a few weeks, the app did what the cassette tapes used to do, and more: a virtual librarian for a forty-year-old machine that captured the synth's screeching tape dumps as audio, decoded them into patches, named them, organized them, sent them back. It mostly worked. Then, a week ago, it mostly stopped working.

The symptoms maddened me in the way only intermittent bugs can. Patches arrived with their sound but not their names. Other times their names arrived orphaned, with no sound. Sequences came back missing pages, different pages each time. The patterns were seemingly random, without rhythm or rhyme. The failures appeared on both my upstairs setup (one synth, one MacBook) and my downstairs setup (another synth, a Mac mini) — two unrelated rigs failing in the same week, which seemed mathematically impossible.

So began the expedition. Claude and I spent a Saturday and Sunday morning blaming everything in sequence. The aging cable. The aging synth. The newer macOS update. The auto-calibration code, the decoder, an obscure interaction between two software features written months apart. Each theory survived a few experiments, then collapsed. We downgraded the app to a version from before any of this could have started; it failed the same way. By Sunday morning we had cornered the problem onto the only remaining suspect: physics itself. The synthesizer was forty-one years old. The analog signal was marginal. Time was passing (see: tears, rain).

Then I listened. Months earlier, during development, I had built a small feature into the app called "what does my tape dump sound like?" — a button that played back the hiss and screech of the 1983 transmission so users could share in the nostalgia, and so they could verify their cable was working. A quirky, sentimental addition. Now it became diagnostic. With the tape dump audible, I heard what Claude couldn't. Tiny interruptions in what should have been a continuous wall of sound. I recorded the same dump in QuickTime, played it back, and heard no gaps at all. Same synth, same cable, same Mac, captured seconds apart — one recording clean, the other punctured with invisible holes.

The cause turned out to be two lines of code in the recording pipeline that asked the Mac to convert audio from one sample rate to another — a conversion the Mac performs in real time, imprecisely, introducing tiny timing errors. The shorter records (individual patches, 286 bits) usually survived. The longer ones (sequence pages, 1463 bits) usually didn't. The synth had transmitted perfectly the whole time. The app had misheard it.

In the end, a musician's ear solved it. Claude had measured the audio every way a computer can measure audio — zero-crossing rates, RMS levels, spectral content, jitter distributions — and found nothing wrong. The signal was, by every quantitative standard, clean. But I'd spent forty years listening to synthesizers. I knew what continuous sound was supposed to feel like, and I could hear when something wasn't continuous. What the measurements missed, the ear caught: a rest where a note should have been. A missing melody inside the screech.

Now I've asked Claude to remove those two lines (in a sea of twenty-three thousand) and tell the app to record at whatever sample rate comes its way: a bug that had hidden in the code for weeks — until a human heard the missing music.
